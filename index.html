Introduction

1.1 Who is a drop-out?

A high school drop-out is a person who does not have a high school diploma and is not enrolled in school. Usually, people aged 20-24 are considered in the calculations of dropout rates in Canada. However, according to a report published by Statistics Canada, “almost all individuals who finish high school have likely done so by the age of 25” [1] . Therefore, on this website, drop-outs will be referred to as individuals aged 25 or older who do not have a high school diploma.

1.2 Dropout in BC

Figure 1 illustrates the dropout rate in BC in the years 2018-2022. The chart demonstrates a positive, declining trend in dropout rates. In fact, the dropout rate in 2022 was only 5 percent. , high school dropout might be seen as an insignificant issue in BC. However, given the fact that nearly 3 million people lived in BC in 2022 (citation), this 5 percent represents almost 150,000 people.

The Cost of Quitting School

Reducing high school dropout rate in BC is essential because dropping out of high school entails negative individual and social consequences.

2.1 Lower earnings

High school drop-outs are the lowest wage earners who live on the edge of, if not in, poverty. In 2021, high school dropouts aged 25-64 earned on average CA$8280 less than those with only a high school diploma. This gap increases to CA$35160 compared to workers with a bachelor’s degree and to CA$48560 compared to those with a master’s degree. Therefore, by dropping out of high school, people deprive themselves of a higher earning potential and a better quality of life.

2.2 Increased crime rate

High school drop-outs are much more likely than graduates to live a life of crime. As illustrated in Figure 3, 75% of inmates in Canada did not have a high school diploma. By dropping out and turning to crime, people not only fail to fulfill their human potential, but also cause harm to their communities.

2.3 Damage to Canadian Economy

According to a 2008 report [4] by Charity Intelligence Canada, every year that produces drop-outs costs Canada CA$5.5 billion through lost income, foregone taxes, and increased spending to address social problems. Today, this value represents CA$7.5 billion. The Canadian government could use this money to improve life standards of all Canadians through investments in healthcare, education, infrastructure, and more.

To this day, there is not a single comprehensive, long-term study that covers the reasons behind high school dropout in British Columbia specifically. However, studies conducted outside of BC provide a valuable insight into the causes of dropout.
